# Resistance Factors: A-01 - Coal Combustion Chain

## Executive Summary

Coal combustion persists despite clear harms due to a mutually reinforcing system of resistance factors: concentrated economic interests ($980B value chain), political power (lobbying, revolving door, regulatory capture), infrastructure lock-in (2,400 GW assets), institutional inertia, and ideological framing (energy security, development rights). Understanding these factors is essential for designing effective interventions.

---

## 1. Economic Resistance Factors

### 1.1 Concentrated Benefits vs. Dispersed Costs

| Aspect | Characteristic | Implication |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| Benefits | Concentrated in few companies, regions, workers | Strong motivation to resist |
| Costs | Dispersed across all populations | Weak motivation to demand change |
| Ratio | Benefits to few, costs to many | Classic collective action problem |
| Result | Organized resistance, disorganized support | Political imbalance |

**Mancur Olson Logic:**
- Coal industry: ~5 million workers globally, $980B revenue, highly organized
- Affected population: ~8 billion people, costs diffuse, poorly organized
- Political outcome: Industry voice dominates despite minority status

### 1.2 Stranded Asset Interests

| Asset Type | Global Value | Owners | Resistance Motivation |
|------------|--------------|--------|----------------------|
| Coal plants | $500-1,000B | Utilities, IPPs | Protect investment recovery |
| Coal mines | $200-400B | Mining companies | Protect revenue stream |
| Rail/port infrastructure | $100-200B | Transport companies | Protect utilization |
| Equipment manufacturers | $50-100B | Industrial companies | Protect orders |

**Resistance Dynamics:**
- Asset owners have strong incentive to delay phase-out
- Each year of delay = additional revenue
- Write-offs hurt stock prices, management bonuses
- Bankruptcy threat motivates political action

### 1.3 Subsidy Dependency

| Subsidy Type | Annual Value | Beneficiaries | Political Power |
|--------------|--------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Production subsidies | $30B | Coal companies | Strong lobby |
| Consumer price support | $50B | Utilities, consumers | Vote-buying |
| Tax breaks | $20B | Coal companies | Tax lobbyists |
| Implicit (externalities) | $6T+ | All coal users | Diffuse |

**Lobbying Tactics:**
- Direct campaign contributions
- Revolving door employment
- Industry-funded research
- Grassroots organization (astroturfing)
- Strategic litigation

### 2.2 Regulatory Capture

| Mechanism | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Personnel | Industry insiders in regulatory positions | EPA administrators, energy ministers |
| Information | Regulators depend on industry data | Emission estimates, cost projections |
| Budget | Industry funds regulatory processes | Permitting fees, legal support |
| Culture | Regulators identify with industry | "Partner" mindset vs. oversight |

**Capture Indicators:**
- Emission standards weaker than health evidence suggests
- Permit approvals despite community opposition
- Enforcement actions rare and weak
- Policies favor incumbents over entrants

### 2.3 Electoral Geography

| Country | Coal Constituencies | Electoral System | Political Leverage |
|---------|---------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| USA | West Virginia, Wyoming, Pennsylvania | Senate overrepresentation | Very High |
| Australia | Queensland, Hunter Valley | Marginal seats | Very High |
| Germany | NRW, Brandenburg, Saxony | Coalition politics | High |
| Poland | Silesia | National identity | Very High |
| India | Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh | State politics | High |

**Geographic Concentration Effect:**
- Coal jobs concentrated in specific regions
- These regions often have outsized electoral influence
- Politicians compete for "coal vote"
- National majority for transition, local majorities against

| Anti-elitism | "War on coal," cultural identity | USA (Appalachia), Australia |

## 3. Social Resistance Factors

### 3.1 Employment and Identity

| Factor | Description | Strength |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Direct employment | 5 million jobs globally | Strong |
| Indirect employment | 15+ million jobs in supply chain | Strong |
| Generational identity | "Coal mining families" | Very Strong |
| Community cohesion | Towns built around coal | Very Strong |
| Skills lock-in | Specialized workforce | Moderate |

**Identity Dimensions:**
- Occupational pride ("We keep the lights on")
- Masculinity norms (dangerous, physical work)
- Family tradition (multi-generation miners)
- Place attachment (mining towns)
- Class consciousness (working class vs. elites)

### 3.2 Fear of Change

| Fear | Basis | Response |
|------|-------|----------|
| Job loss | Real historical precedent | Just transition guarantees |
| Community decline | Observed in past transitions | Regional development |
| Loss of status | Identity threat | Dignified transition |
| Economic uncertainty | Real concerns | Financial security |

### 3.3 Distrust

| Target of Distrust | Basis | Manifestation |
|-------------------|-------|---------------|
| Government | Broken promises (past transitions) | Skepticism of pledges |
| Environmentalists | Perceived anti-worker | Polarization |
| "Elites" | Economic marginalization | Populist politics |
| Science | Perceived bias | Climate skepticism |

## 4. Institutional Resistance Factors

### 4.1 Infrastructure Lock-In

| Infrastructure | Lifetime | Sunk Cost | Lock-In Strength |
|----------------|----------|-----------|------------------|
| Coal plants | 40-60 years | $1-5B each | Very Strong |
| Mines | 30-50 years | Variable | Strong |
| Rail lines | 50-100 years | $100M-1B | Strong |
| Port facilities | 50+ years | $500M-2B | Moderate |
| Grid connections | 30-50 years | Variable | Moderate |

**Lock-In Dynamics:**
```text
Initial Investment â†’ Sunk Cost â†’ Pressure to Utilize â†’ Extended Operation â†’ More Lock-In
```

### 4.2 Institutional Inertia

| Institution | Inertia Factor | Resistance Mechanism |
|-------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Utilities | Return on rate base | Slow investment shifts |
| Regulators | Established procedures | Process delays |
| Grid operators | Legacy systems | Integration challenges |

| Mining leases | Extraction rights | Strong |
| Supply contracts | Coal delivery commitments | Moderate |
| Labor agreements | Employment guarantees | Moderate |
| International investment treaties | Investor-state dispute | Strong |

**ISDS Risk:**

## 5. Knowledge/Information Resistance

### 5.1 Manufactured Doubt

| Tactic | Mechanism | Effectiveness |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Fund contrarian research | Challenge scientific consensus | Declining |
| Emphasize uncertainty | "More research needed" | Moderate |
| Attack scientists | Personal and professional | Limited |
| Alternative facts | Industry-funded think tanks | Regional |

**Key Organizations:**
- Heartland Institute
- Global Warming Policy Foundation
- CO2 Coalition
- Various national equivalents

### 5.2 Information Asymmetry

| Dimension | Industry Advantage | Counter |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| Technical data | Owns production/emission data | Mandatory disclosure |
| Cost estimates | Proprietary | Independent analysis |
| Alternatives assessment | Self-serving projections | Public research |
| Job impact claims | Inflated | Fact-checking |

### 5.3 Narrative Control

| Narrative | Function | Counter-Narrative |
|-----------|----------|------------------|
| "Clean coal" | Delay, false hope | Coal cannot be clean |
| "Baseload necessity" | Grid reliability fear | Storage and flexibility |
| "Development right" | Equity framing | Clean energy for development |
| "Energy security" | National security fear | Domestic renewables |
| "Affordability" | Consumer cost fear | External costs, falling renewable costs |
