# Precedents: A-01 - Coal Combustion Chain

## Executive Summary

Numerous interventions have been attempted to reduce coal combustion harms, ranging from end-of-pipe controls to complete phase-outs. The UK's coal exit demonstrates that rapid phase-out is possible; Germany's shows transition can protect workers; China's shows air quality can motivate action. Key lessons: economics drives change, phase-out dates create certainty, and political will is the binding constraint.

## 2. Failed or Insufficient Interventions

### 2.1 Failed Policies

#### Australian Carbon Price (2012-2014)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Timeline | Introduced July 2012, repealed July 2014 |
| Mechanism | Fixed price ($23/tonne) transitioning to ETS |
| Results | Emissions dropped 1.4% (economy grew 5%) |
| Failure cause | Political reversal (Abbott government) |
| Key factors | Lack of bipartisan support, fossil fuel lobby |

**Lessons:**
- Carbon pricing vulnerable to political reversal
- Industry opposition can overwhelm policy
- Need durable institutional design
- Bipartisan consensus essential for longevity

#### EU ETS Phase 1 (2005-2007)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Timeline | First compliance period |
| Mechanism | Cap-and-trade for power and industry |
| Results | Prices collapsed to near zero |
| Failure cause | Over-allocation of permits |
| Key factors | Industry lobbying, national allocation plans |

**Lessons:**
- Cap must be binding (scarcity creates price)
- Industry will game allocation if possible
- Need robust monitoring and adjustment mechanisms
- Centralized cap-setting better than national

### 2.2 Insufficient Interventions

#### Voluntary Corporate Commitments

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Mechanism | Net-zero pledges, RE100, etc. |
| Results | Symbolic progress, limited real impact |
| Limitation | Scope 3 excluded, offsetting, delays |
| Key factors | Lack of accountability, greenwashing |

**Lessons:**
- Voluntary alone is insufficient
- Need mandatory disclosure and standards
- Scope 3 inclusion essential
- Third-party verification required

#### Clean Coal Technologies

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Mechanism | CCS, HELE (high efficiency, low emissions) |
| Results | Minimal deployment, cost escalation |
| Investment | $10+ billion in CCS with few successes |
| Key factors | Cost, complexity, continued CO2 problem |

**Lessons:**
- CCS has not delivered at scale
- "Clean coal" largely a delay tactic
- Alternative technologies now cheaper
- Efficiency improvements insufficient

## 3. Historical Analogies

### 3.1 Successful Transitions

#### Tobacco Control

| Aspect | Tobacco | Coal Parallel |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Timeline | 1960s evidence â†’ 1990s+ action | 1990s evidence â†’ 2020s+ action |
| Mechanism | Litigation, regulation, taxation | Carbon pricing, regulation, liability |
| Industry response | Denial, delay, diversification | Denial, delay, diversification |
| Tipping point | Master Settlement (1998) | Pending |

**Lessons:**
- Scientific evidence alone doesn't change policy
- Litigation can break industry political power
- Public health framing effective
- Took decades but ultimately successful

#### Ozone Depletion (CFCs)

| Aspect | CFCs | Coal Parallel |
|--------|------|---------------|
| Timeline | 1974 evidence â†’ 1987 Montreal Protocol | 1990s evidence â†’ Paris Agreement |
| Mechanism | International treaty, technology substitution | International treaties, technology substitution |
| Industry response | Initial denial, then cooperation | Ongoing denial, some cooperation |
| Key difference | Fewer affected industries, cheaper alternatives | Massive industry, energy security framing |

**Lessons:**
- International cooperation possible
- Technology alternatives enable action
- Industry cooperation accelerates transition
- Smaller problem = easier solution

### 3.2 Relevant Historical Transitions

#### Horse to Automobile (1900-1920)

| Aspect | Transition Lesson |
|--------|-------------------|

| Mechanism | Superior technology, cost decline |
| Displaced industry | Horses, stables, blacksmiths |
| Key factor | Consumer preference + infrastructure |

**Lesson:** Technology transitions can be rapid when alternatives are superior

#### Whale Oil to Petroleum (1850-1870)

| Aspect | Transition Lesson |
|--------|-------------------|
| Timeline | ~20 years |
| Mechanism | Petroleum cheaper, more abundant |
| Environmental benefit | Whales not hunted to extinction |
| Key factor | Economic competition |

**Lesson:** Economic superiority drives transition without regulation

## 5. Lessons Learned

### 5.1 What Works

| Factor | Evidence | Applicability |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| Binding phase-out dates | UK, Ontario success | Universal |
| Economic competition | US market decline | Where alternatives cheap |
| Air quality framing | China, Ontario | High pollution contexts |
| Carbon pricing (high enough) | UK, EU (reformed) | Politically feasible contexts |
| Stakeholder process | Germany commission | Democratic contexts |
| Litigation | Tobacco precedent | Strong legal systems |

### 5.2 What Doesn't Work

| Factor | Evidence | Why Fails |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Voluntary commitments | Corporate pledges | No accountability |
| Low carbon prices | China ETS, early EU | No behavioral change |
| Technology optimism | CCS failure | Cheaper alternatives exist |
| Gradual transition alone | Emissions still rising | Too slow for climate |
| Ignoring workers | Appalachian decline | Political backlash |
